# How I Decide Whether an Idea Is Worth Building

I have more ideas than I have time to build.

Some arrive while I am using a frustrating tool. Others come from watching someone repeat a task that should be easier. A few sound exciting for a day and lose their value as soon as I examine them closely.

The difficult part is rarely finding an idea. It is deciding which idea deserves months of work.

Building too quickly can feel productive, but code is not proof that a product should exist. A polished interface can still solve the wrong problem. A long feature list can hide the fact that nobody urgently needs the result.

Before I build, I try to answer a simpler question:

> Is this only an interesting idea, or is it a real problem worth solving?

I do not use a perfect formula. I use a practical decision process that helps me slow down, challenge my assumptions, and decide what deserves my attention.

## I start with the problem, not the product

An idea usually arrives in the form of a solution:

- “I should build an app for this.”
- “This needs a dashboard.”
- “AI could automate that.”
- “This could become a marketplace.”

Those statements begin too late. They describe what I could build, not why it should exist.

I go backward and write the problem in plain language. If I cannot explain it without mentioning a feature or technology, I probably do not understand it yet.

A useful problem statement answers four questions:

1. Who experiences the problem?
2. What are they trying to accomplish?
3. What makes it difficult today?
4. What happens if the problem remains unsolved?

“Small service businesses need a better dashboard” is weak.

“Small service businesses lose track of leads and follow-up opportunities because their client information is spread across messages, spreadsheets, and memory” is stronger. It identifies the user, the situation, and the cost.

The clearer the problem becomes, the easier it is to judge the idea.

## I look for evidence that the problem is real

My own frustration is useful evidence, but it is not enough by itself.

I look for repetition. Are other people experiencing the same problem? How are they describing it? What are they already doing to work around it?

Strong signals include:

- People repeatedly asking the same question.
- Businesses using spreadsheets, messages, or manual work to fill a gap.
- Someone paying for a poor solution because the problem matters.
- Several products solving part of the problem but leaving an important step unfinished.
- A mistake that regularly costs time, money, customers, or opportunities.

The workaround is especially important. People do not always say, “I need a new product.” They show the need through what they already do.

If someone maintains three spreadsheets, copies information between systems, and sets personal reminders to keep the process working, that behavior tells me more than a casual compliment about my idea.

I also pay attention to weak evidence:

- Friends saying the idea sounds good.
- Social media likes.
- A large market with no specific user in mind.
- Competitors receiving attention.
- Excitement about a technology rather than the outcome.

Interest is not the same as need.

## I identify the first person the product is for

“Everyone” is not a useful first customer.

When a product tries to serve everyone at the beginning, the language becomes vague, the features multiply, and the experience becomes less useful for the people with the strongest need.

I choose one clear starting user. Not the only person who may ever use the product—the first person it must serve exceptionally well.

I want to know:

- What kind of work do they do?
- What are they trying to achieve right now?
- What do they currently use?
- What part of the process causes the most friction?
- What would make them trust a new solution?

This does not require an elaborate customer profile with invented personal details. I need enough specificity to make good product decisions.

If I can picture the exact situation in which someone reaches for the product, the idea becomes easier to test.

## I define the useful result

Features are easy to imagine. Results are harder—and more important.

Before creating a feature list, I complete this sentence:

> After using this product, the user can ________.

The answer should describe a meaningful change.

For example:

- Know what is preventing the business from being contract-ready.
- See which existing clients have additional service opportunities.
- Evaluate a YouTube niche before investing months of work.
- Manage a dispatch workflow without losing critical information.

These are results. They create a standard against which every proposed feature can be judged.

If a feature does not help produce the main result, it probably does not belong in the first version.

## I study what people use now

Competition does not automatically make an idea bad. In many cases, it confirms that people care about the problem.

I study direct competitors, but I also study substitutes:

- Spreadsheets
- Notes
- Email
- Messaging apps
- Manual services
- A combination of several unrelated tools
- Doing nothing

The real competitor is often the current habit, not another startup.

I ask:

- What does the existing solution do well?
- Where does it become confusing or expensive?
- Who is it designed for?
- Who does it overlook?
- Why do people continue using it despite its weaknesses?
- What would need to be meaningfully better for someone to switch?

I do not want to build the same product with different colors. There should be a clear reason for the new product to exist.

That reason could be a more focused audience, a simpler workflow, better guidance, more relevant language, faster setup, or an outcome competitors do not make visible.

Different is not enough. The difference must matter.

## I reduce the idea to its smallest useful version

The first version should not prove that I can build everything. It should prove that the core result is valuable.

I ask:

> What is the smallest complete experience that solves the central problem?

“Small” does not mean careless or unfinished. It means focused.

A focused first version should:

- Solve one important problem from beginning to end.
- Be understandable without a long explanation.
- Produce a result the user can recognize.

It does not need every integration, automation, role, report, or customization I can imagine.

If removing a feature destroys the main result, the feature may be essential. If the result remains intact, the feature can wait.

This step protects the product from becoming a collection of possibilities instead of a useful tool.

## I calculate the real cost of building it

The cost of an idea is larger than the initial build.

There is also:

- Testing
- Content and data preparation
- Security
- Customer support
- Payment processing
- Legal and privacy requirements
- Hosting and external services
- Bug fixes
- Documentation
- Ongoing maintenance
- The cost of not working on something else

An idea may be useful and still be the wrong project for me to build now.

I ask whether I have the time, knowledge, access, and patience to support it after launch. If the product depends on information I cannot reliably obtain, an integration I cannot access, or operations I cannot maintain, that changes the decision.

The goal is not to eliminate uncertainty. The goal is to see the commitment honestly.

## I think about distribution before development

A good product does not automatically find users.

Before building, I ask how the first ten people will discover it. Then I ask how the next hundred could arrive.

Possible paths include:

- An existing audience
- Search demand
- Communities where the problem is already discussed
- Direct outreach to a clearly defined group
- Partnerships
- Educational content
- Referrals created by the product’s result

“I will post it online” is not a distribution plan.

If I cannot identify a credible path to the first users, I do not necessarily abandon the idea. But I treat distribution as part of the product problem—not something to solve after months of development.

## I use a simple decision test

After the research, I summarize the idea on one page and rate it against seven questions:

1. **Problem:** Is the problem specific and costly enough to matter?
2. **Evidence:** Have I seen real behavior that confirms it?
3. **User:** Do I know exactly who the first version is for?
4. **Result:** Can I state the useful outcome clearly?
5. **Difference:** Is there a meaningful reason to choose this solution?
6. **Scope:** Can I build a focused first version with the resources I have?
7. **Reach:** Do I have a believable way to reach the first users?

I rate each one from 0 to 2:

- **0:** Unknown or unsupported
- **1:** Plausible, but needs stronger evidence
- **2:** Clear and supported

The score does not make the decision for me. It reveals where I am relying on hope.

A low score does not always mean “never.” It often means “not yet.” I may need more conversations, a smaller scope, a clearer user, or a better way to reach the market.

## I test the riskiest assumption first

Every idea contains assumptions. One of them is usually more dangerous than the others.

It might be:

- People care enough to change their current behavior.
- The required data is available.
- Users understand the scoring method.
- Businesses will trust the recommendation.
- The workflow can be simplified without removing essential control.
- The audience can afford the price.

I identify the assumption that could make the entire product fail and test that before polishing everything else.

The test may be a conversation, a manual service, a clickable prototype, a sample report, a landing page, or a small working tool. The correct test depends on the uncertainty.

I do not need to pretend the product already exists. I need to learn whether the central promise is strong enough to continue.

## When I decide to build

I move forward when I can say:

- The problem is clear.
- The need appears in real behavior, not only opinions.
- I know who I am serving first.
- The product creates a result people can recognize.
- The difference is meaningful.
- The first version is focused enough to finish.
- I have a realistic path to users.
- I understand the largest risk and know how to test it.

I still may be wrong. Every build contains uncertainty.

But the purpose of this process is not to guarantee success. It is to make a better decision before spending months turning an assumption into software.

Ideas are easy to collect. Useful products require judgment, restraint, and a willingness to solve the real problem instead of building the most impressive version of the idea.

That is how I decide what deserves to become real.

## A one-page idea check

Before you start building, write down:

- **The user:** Who experiences the problem most clearly?
- **The situation:** When and where does it happen?
- **The problem:** What is difficult, slow, costly, or unreliable?
- **The current workaround:** What do people use today?
- **The consequence:** What happens when the problem is not solved?
- **The outcome:** What can the user do after using your solution?
- **The difference:** Why should this solution exist?
- **The smallest useful version:** What is essential for the result?
- **The biggest assumption:** What must be true for the idea to work?
- **The first test:** What is the fastest honest way to test that assumption?
- **The first users:** How will you reach them?
- **The commitment:** What will this require after launch?

If several answers are still vague, do not add features. Improve the answers.
